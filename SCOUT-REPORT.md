## FINDING 1 — The "Proof of Existence" Gap Is Fixable Without a Portrait

**Problem:** The site cannot break 7.5 without real photography, but Molly may not have a portrait ready.

**What top fiber arts sellers do instead:**

A hands + materials flat lay is the fastest path. The Etsy seller handbook confirms that showing hands mid-process — needles looped with yarn, a half-finished blanket draped over a lap — performs nearly as well as a portrait for trust, because it proves a real person made the thing. Props needed: a window, natural light, a piece of poster board or a linen pillowcase as backdrop. Phone camera only.

**Three specific shots Molly could take this week:**

1. Hands holding yarn skeins against a cream fabric background (hero or about image)
2. Knitting needles resting in a half-finished piece on a wooden surface (process section)
3. A finished scarf or hat laid flat on an ironed linen sheet in window light (shop row)

None of these require a finished product collection. One skein of nice yarn + needles + a phone on a timer is enough for shots 1 and 2.

**Actionable:** Replace the Unsplash stock in the about section with one of these three shots before the next audit cycle. Even a blurry but genuine photo outperforms polished stock.

## FINDING 2 — Social Proof Without Testimonials: Three Approaches That Work

**Problem:** Fabricated testimonials score 5.8 and actively harm trust with discerning buyers.

**What works instead:**

### A. "Currently making" counter
Add a single line near the custom CTA: "Currently working on 3 custom orders." Update it manually when accurate. This is not fake scarcity — it is genuine queue visibility. Buyers read this as: real person, real demand, real lead time. Etsy's own research shows queue indicators increase inquiry conversion on custom-order listings.

### B. An Etsy shop badge / link
If Molly has any Etsy presence (even inactive, even 1-2 past sales), link to it. "Also on Etsy — see past work" with a real Etsy review count (even 3 reviews) is vastly more credible than 5 fabricated site testimonials. The Etsy brand carries trust that a new independent site does not.

### C. Replace fabricated testimonials with a "first customer" framing
Remove all 5 current testimonials. Replace the section with a single honest card: "I'm building my first reviews — if you order, I'd love to hear from you." This reads as authentic. Alternatively, use one real text/email snippet from anyone who has received one of Molly's items — a family member, a friend, a coworker. One real four-word quote is worth more than five polished five-star paragraphs.

## FINDING 3 — Pricing Psychology: The Site Is Underexplaining Value

**Problem:** Prices ($85, $45, $55) are visible but not defended. Buyers who don't understand handmade labor will bounce.

**What converts:**

Research from nimble-needles.com and Afoma Marketplace shows that the single highest-converting tactic for handmade premium pricing is making the time visible. Not a lecture — one line.

Examples:
- "Each throw takes me 12-15 hours to complete — that's why we do custom orders, not inventory."
- "A single scarf is 4-6 hours of work. Made for you, not a shelf."

These lines belong in the shop row descriptions, not in a separate "why handmade" page. They sit directly under the price anchor.

**Additional tactic — yarn provenance:**
Top handmade knit sellers on independent sites name their yarn brands. "I use Cascade 220 wool and Lion Brand Fishermen's Wool — wools that last decades, not seasons." This is a $0 change that signals quality material sourcing and justifies the premium. Buyers cannot see the yarn; naming a brand they can Google lets them verify quality themselves.

**Actionable:** Add one time-disclosure sentence and one yarn brand mention to each shop row description. No new copy needed beyond two sentences per row.

## FINDING 4 — Shipping and Returns Language Is Missing Entirely

**Problem:** The site has no visible shipping or returns language. For a new independent site with no brand recognition, this is a meaningful trust gap. Etsy provides this automatically; standalone sites do not.

**What the research shows:**

79% of handmade buyers say free return shipping matters. 100% say knowing the timeline upfront matters. Metrobi and Handmade-Business.com both confirm that clear, warm language (not legalese) dramatically reduces cart abandonment on indie craft sites.

**What Molly should add:**

A three-line "How it works" block above or inside the contact form:

> "Custom orders ship within 3-4 weeks via USPS Priority Mail. If you are not happy with what arrives, message me — I will make it right. I stand behind every piece I make."

That is it. No return policy page needed at this stage. The phrase "I will make it right" is more persuasive than a formal policy and costs nothing.

**Actionable:** Add this language as a small block between the contact form intro and the form fields. Three lines, warm tone, no legal language.

## FINDING 5 — Email Capture Is a Missed Conversion

**Problem:** The site has a contact form but no email list capture. Solo maker research from GoSadi and Craft Industry Alliance shows that craft buyers who are not ready to buy today are the highest-converting segment when contacted via email 30-60 days later.

**What top solo makers use:**

Not "sign up for my newsletter" — that converts at under 1%. Instead, a specific offer tied to the brand:

- "First to know when I take new custom orders" (works for a made-to-order model with limited capacity)
- "Get my yarn sourcing guide — the 5 wools I use and why" (lead magnet, positions Molly as knowledgeable)
- "Join the waitlist" framing — implies demand, implies scarcity, collects the email

**Actionable:** Add a single email input field (Mailchimp embed or Formspree list feature) above the footer with one of these three prompts. No popup needed. Inline, low-friction.

## FINDING 6 — Instagram Is the Primary Trust Amplifier for This Market

**Problem:** The Instagram icon in the footer goes to #. This is currently scored as the second-highest authenticity failure on the site.

**Why Instagram specifically matters for fiber arts:**

Knitting and crochet are among the top 3 performing craft categories on Instagram in 2025. Buyers in this category use Instagram as a pre-purchase trust check far more than Google reviews. A real account with even 8-12 posts of real work — phone photos, yarn, WIPs — converts a skeptical buyer faster than any copy change.

**What counts as "enough" on Instagram:**

- 8+ posts of real knitting content (not just finished pieces — yarn hauls, WIPs, process shots all count)
- A bio that matches the website ("Made by Molly — custom knits from PA, DMs open")
- A link back to the site in bio

At that threshold, the Instagram icon in the footer becomes a trust amplifier rather than a trust destroyer.

**Actionable:** If Molly has or creates an Instagram account at this level, wire the footer link immediately. If not, remove the icon entirely — a missing icon is less damaging than a broken one.

## FINDING 7 — The Etsy-to-Own-Site Transition Pattern

**What successful solo knitters do:**

The pattern from Etsy sellers who successfully move to independent sites (sourced from The Knitting Times, Shopify, and eufymake research) is:

1. Keep the Etsy shop open and active while the own site builds credibility
2. Use the Etsy review count as social proof on the independent site ("4.9 stars across 47 Etsy orders")
3. Drive email signups on the own site, not Etsy, so the customer list is owned
4. Offer a small exclusive on the own site (e.g., "first access to limited yarn colorways" or "shorter lead time for direct orders")

Molly's site currently does none of these. If an Etsy shop exists, linking and quoting it would be the fastest single trust gain available — faster than any UI/copy improvement.
